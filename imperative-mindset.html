How to Switch from the Imperative Mindset

November 10, 2015

Summary: **

One of the most often-asked questions budding functional programmers ask me is "how do I swatch from the imperative mindset?". They've learned to program in one paradigm and the switch to another is difficult. All of their habits of coding are now not helpful. What new habits should they learn?

The imperative paradigm is quite intuitive in it's way. In it, programs are list of instructions to perform, one at a time, in order. Each instruction modifies the state of the computer in some way, or performs some action outside of the computer, until, after the final action, the answer you were calculating is there, or the effect you wanted has been achieved. The email has been sent, the missile has been launched, etc.

You can think of imperative programming as giving instructions to someone who is changing a tire. First, get out the tire iron and spare. Then, get out the jack. Next, jack up the car, etc. Each step changes the world in some way, and at the end of the process, the tire has been changed.

Now, obviously programs in a functional language do have to have effects, and those effects have to be in the right order. This kind of step-by-step instruction programming is necessary and entirely possible in Haskell and Clojure. In Haskell, you have the IO type, which represents actions like "jack up the car". And in Clojure, you can always perform an effect as part of any function. So a job like changing the tire might actually be programmed entirely in an imperative way. That would be fine and very common, actually.

But let's do a little thought experiment. Let's say that as we gave instructions to the person changing the tire, instead of performing them as we gave them, she just listened carefully until all of the instructions were given, then executed them. It becomes clear, with this example, that the instructions are data. They are information given to the worker, who can remember them, then execute them at a later time.

Well, if she can remember them, why not do something more interesting? Why can't she try to optimize them? Maybe she can choose a different order.
